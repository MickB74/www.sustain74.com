SBTi’s Scope 3 Shake‑Up: How Suppliers & Climate Solution Providers Can Get Ahead

August 10, 2025

SBTi’s draft Corporate Net‑Zero Standard V2 (Mar 2025)[1][2] strengthens supply‑chain accountability. Here’s what’s changing, why it matters for suppliers, and how credits/SAF/clean‑energy providers can position for value—backed by SBTi and company sources.

On this page

    What Is Scope 3?

    Scope 3 includes all indirect GHG emissions that occur in a company’s value chain, both upstream and downstream. It often makes up the largest share of a company’s total carbon footprint[3].

    Scope 3 Categories (GHG Protocol) [3]

    # Category Description
    1 Purchased goods & services[3] Cradle‑to‑gate emissions from the extraction, production and transport of goods and services purchased by the reporting company.
    2 Capital goods[3] Emissions from the production of capitalized goods (e.g., buildings, machinery, vehicles) acquired by the company.
    3 Fuel‑ and energy‑related activities (not in Scopes 1 or 2)[3] Upstream emissions related to fuels and electricity purchased and consumed (e.g., extraction, production and transmission losses).
    4 Upstream transportation & distribution[3] Third‑party transportation and warehousing of purchased products between suppliers and the reporting company.
    5 Waste generated in operations[3] Emissions from third‑party disposal and treatment of waste generated by the company’s operations.
    6 Business travel[3] Transportation of employees for business‑related activities in vehicles not owned or controlled by the company.
    7 Employee commuting[3] Transportation of employees between their homes and worksites.
    8 Upstream leased assets[3] Operation of assets leased by the company (upstream) not included in Scopes 1 and 2.
    9 Downstream transportation & distribution[3] Transportation and distribution of sold products in vehicles and facilities not owned or controlled by the company, including retail and warehousing.
    10 Processing of sold products[3] Emissions from processing of intermediate products sold by the company by third parties.
    11 Use of sold products[3] Direct use‑phase emissions from goods and services sold by the company.
    12 End‑of‑life treatment of sold products[3] Waste disposal and treatment of products sold at end of life.
    13 Downstream leased assets[3] Operation of assets owned by the reporting company and leased to other entities.
    14 Franchises[3] Operation of franchises not included in Scopes 1 and 2 of the franchisor.
    15 Investments[3] Emissions from equity and debt investments and project finance; primary category for financial institutions.

    What’s changing in SBTi V2 (Draft, Mar 2025)

    See also SBTi’s overview page: Developing the Corporate Net‑Zero Standard V2.[4]
    Why this matters for suppliers
    • Buyers will prioritize high‑impact categories and expect activity‑based, auditable data.
    • Many suppliers will be asked to set SBT‑aligned targets and show verified progress.
    • Readiness will influence RFP scoring, renewals, and preferred‑supplier status.
    Opportunity for solution providers
    • Bundle credits/SAF/renewables/efficiency with verifiable emissions data.
    • Map offerings to new alignment metrics (e.g., net‑zero supplier spend).
    • Enable procurement‑led decarbonization with robust MRV (measurement, reporting, verification).
    How to prepare (suppliers)
    1. Materiality check: Are your offerings in customer categories > 5% of Scope 3?
    2. Upgrade data: Shift from spend‑based proxies to activity‑based, auditable data; plan a multi‑year data‑quality roadmap.
    3. Set targets: Adopt SBT‑aligned goals and document progress with evidence.
    How to win (solution providers)
    1. Bundle solutions + MRV: Credits/SAF/renewables paired with verifiable data.
    2. Map to V2 metrics: Help buyers increase % spend with net‑zero‑aligned suppliers.
    3. Procurement enablement: Tooling, attestations, and supplier engagement content.
